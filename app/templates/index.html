The Deliverable

Our algorithm has learned to identify, based on features of it's own choosing, which historic style of painting an image resembles.

Our project is designed to assist artists and art viewers in identifying qualities about an artwork through recognizing the period the artwork is from. By training our model with a dataset of thousands of images sourced from the National Gallery of Art, we were able to create a project that can accurately identify an artwork to be one of five art styles, ranging from Renaissance to Realist. These results can be utilized by artists to better analyze their work, as well as enabling viewers to quickly and easily learn more about the artworks themselves.

Why did we do it?

The Why

AI allows for limitless possibility. So how did we narrow down our product idea? What roadblocks did we run into that shifted the course of the project?

We believed it would be fascinating to see how an AI distinguishes painting styles and what styles it might classify unknown painting. Take a kindergardener's creatively abstract masterpiece, which historic style of painting does it most closely resemble? Our tool 
could even be used by people to identify the styles of paintings they might find in a museum or art gallery!

We had originally planned on making a project that could distinguish dense and sparsely filled areas to assist officials in improving how we social distance during the pandemic, but we soon ran into trouble finding images due to privacy issues and low numbers of useable images. We decided to step back and rethink, which led us to develop the idea of making a tool that can identify different painting styles.

The How: Method

Collection, Optimization, Development, Presentation

The first step was to collect thousands of images, many of which we pulled from the National Gallery of Art's webpage. After collection, we built and ran Python scripts to cut out any duplicates from our dataset, funnel each of our collections of images into one folder, and then split that folder into three: one for training our AI, one for validating it's accuracy, and one for testing.

These three folders were then passed to Yolo, an open-source algorithm that uses muliple neural networks to provide accurate object detection and localization. Once we finished training our weights, we ran our algorithm and saw our results (shown to the right). With the program completed, it was time for the front end (the webpage).

We built our webpage from the ground up, putting together what you see before you. Once the basic HTML was completed (the appearance of the website), we began integration with Python, using a module called Flask to tie our machine-learning program to the website. After some polishing touches, it was ready for publication. 

Our confusion matrix (shown above) shows the accuracy of our algorithm. Here it shows 75% accuracy in recognizing Impressionist-era paintings, 84% accuracy with Baroque, 97% for Spanish Southwest, 87% for Renaissance, and 79% for Realist paintings.
